# Problem-Solving Skills Hierarchy

This file organizes problem-solving skills from broad worldview and mindset down to detailed methods and tools.

## 1. Worldview and Orientation

- `systems-thinking` — seeing problems as part of larger systems with inputs, outputs, constraints, and feedback loops
- `first-principles-thinking` — reducing a problem to fundamental truths instead of relying only on analogy
- `probabilistic-thinking` — reasoning under uncertainty using likelihoods, confidence, and trade-offs
- `long-term-thinking` — considering downstream effects, maintainability, and compounding consequences
- `interdisciplinary-thinking` — borrowing models and approaches from multiple domains
- `reality-based-thinking` — grounding decisions in evidence, constraints, and observations

## 2. Core Mental Habits

- `curiosity` — wanting to understand what is really happening
- `clarity-seeking` — defining terms, goals, assumptions, and scope
- `intellectual-humility` — accepting uncertainty and correcting weak models
- `patience` — resisting premature conclusions
- `persistence` — continuing through ambiguity and setbacks
- `adaptability` — changing strategy when evidence suggests a better path
- `precision` — being careful in language, logic, and distinctions
- `decision-discipline` — knowing when to stop analyzing and choose

## 3. Problem Framing

- `problem-definition` — clearly stating what is wrong, for whom, and compared to what target state
- `goal-decomposition` — separating primary goals, secondary goals, and constraints
- `boundary-setting` — defining what is in scope and out of scope
- `constraint-identification` — identifying limits in time, money, regulation, technology, or people
- `stakeholder-mapping` — identifying affected parties, decision-makers, and implementers
- `reframing` — checking whether the problem is really technical, organizational, measurement-related, or symptomatic
- `abstraction-control` — moving up for principles and down for implementation details

## 4. Analysis

- `root-cause-analysis` — finding causes rather than treating symptoms
- `causal-reasoning` — distinguishing correlation from causation
- `pattern-recognition` — identifying repeated structures, bottlenecks, and failure modes
- `comparative-analysis` — comparing current vs target, before vs after, option A vs option B
- `decomposition` — breaking large problems into smaller subproblems
- `prioritization` — focusing on high-leverage factors
- `quantitative-reasoning` — using estimates, calculations, and simple models
- `diagnostic-reasoning` — narrowing plausible explanations using evidence

## 5. Solution Generation

- `divergent-thinking` — generating multiple options before selecting one
- `analogy` — applying patterns from similar problems or other fields
- `constraint-based-creativity` — using limits as design guidance
- `combination-thinking` — merging partial ideas into stronger solutions
- `inversion` — asking how the problem could fail or worsen
- `simplification` — removing unnecessary complexity
- `alternative-generation` — always creating more than one plausible path

## 6. Evaluation and Judgment

- `trade-off-analysis` — understanding what is gained and lost with each option
- `cost-benefit-analysis` — comparing value, effort, and downside
- `risk-assessment` — evaluating probability, impact, detectability, and mitigation
- `sensitivity-analysis` — testing which assumptions matter most
- `scenario-planning` — checking best-case, base-case, and worst-case outcomes
- `robustness-evaluation` — preferring solutions that still work under messy conditions
- `reversibility-awareness` — favoring reversible decisions when uncertainty is high
- `opportunity-cost-thinking` — recognizing what is sacrificed by choosing one option

## 7. Execution

- `planning` — turning solutions into concrete steps and milestones
- `sequencing` — doing tasks in the right order
- `resource-allocation` — assigning time, people, attention, and tools effectively
- `experimentation` — piloting, prototyping, and testing before scaling
- `monitoring` — tracking progress and signals
- `troubleshooting` — responding to deviations during implementation
- `iteration` — improving through repeated refinement
- `closure` — confirming the problem is actually solved

## 8. Communication and Collaboration

- `clear-communication` — making the problem and solution understandable
- `questioning` — asking useful, revealing questions
- `listening` — absorbing objections, weak signals, and domain expertise
- `alignment-building` — getting people to agree on the problem and path
- `negotiation` — balancing competing interests and constraints
- `facilitation` — helping groups think effectively together
- `documentation` — recording assumptions, decisions, and rationale
- `escalation-judgment` — knowing when a problem needs higher authority or broader coordination
